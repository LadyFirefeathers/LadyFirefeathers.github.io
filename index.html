Project 1: Doughnut Delight
Timeframe: one hour
Team: Two UX-ers and a friend
Challenge: Create a responsive website for a doughnut company that has a food truck and also will deliver custom made doughnuts to your office
  1. First we established that the site would have the goal of delighting board office workers who are looking for a tasty treat to brighton their day.
  2. On the home page we placed a map that would always show where the truck would be parked and if it was open for business. Then we added a page containing the feed from a live web cam in the kitchen of the bakery. Finally we gave the user the ability to visually create their own custom doughnut and order a dozen to be delivered to their office.
  3. As we talked, a low-fidelity wireframe for both mobile and web was sketched out on the table. (General Assembly is furnished with tables especially made to use dry-erase markers so no tables were damaged in the making of these wireframes.)
Project 2: Updated UX for Intrepid Travel
Timeframe: Two weeks
Team: Three UX-ers
Challenge: How do you take a site with hundreds of prepackaged trips and make it pleasant to use by age 50+ people of the western world?
  1. Partnering with two of my classmates, Katie and Emerson, we first tackled this project by interviewing the age 50+ people in our networks about their travel habits. We created an infinity diagram and concluded that Trust is the key to this demographic. Generally speaking we found that the 50+ demographic is adventurous in travel but they don’t trust the internet as a whole.
  2. I contributed to the project by focusing on creating trust though a visually simple and easy to use interface on the home page. I started designing with in the size confines of mobile and thinking as we age our motor-skills become less preses and hence I wanted big, definitive buttons. Also, our eyesight starts to become compromised so I thought about large text as well. Low fidelity wireframes were ideated.
  3. Next I expanded the block idea to tablet and desktop. I bumped into the fact that with seven continents there is always an empty space. At first my group filled this with a list of themed trips however when we tested our first prototype, this confused people since themes weren’t a continent or a place to go. About Me

My professional story starts in 1999 when I graduated from a two-year intensive design school with a degree in Graphic Design. Over the years and through a minor and major recession I received my bachelor of Arts and found my niche in long document layout (catalogs, magazines, and reports). I was introduced to User Experience as a field in December 2014 and fell head over heals in love. Looking into how to give this new love a direction and foundation I decide to attend General Assemble's User Experience Immersion. Now I'm trekking on, finding my passion growing with every UX project and peer I meet.

The project featured above in the "my process" section are ones I acomplished during my ten-week immersive class at General Assembly. Here are general descriptions of several other projects I worked on:

General Assembly User Experience Immersion | April-June 2015

E-commerce Site Design Project- Created an e-commerce site for a school uniform company. Identified through user research that customers wanted a master list of clothing requirements. Devised a path for users to enter the name of a school and have the requirements list follow them through out their shopping experience. Demonstrated solution through a medium fidelity clickable prototype.
Site Improvement Project- A travel company wanted to step into the 50+ age market. Working with a group of three UX-ers, researched this age bracket, then streamlined both general search functionality and comparison feature. Tested site using medium fidelity prototype, then created hi fidelity wireframs to present.
List App Creation Project- Together with two classmates and three developers, designed the concept for a movie listing app that focused on giving a rewarding feeling to the user. Created persona, and scenario, then low-fidelity wireframes keeping developer ability and time in mind. Presented a high fidelity prototype to class and faculty.

Examples of my Work Experience

I've worked as a graphic designer and production artist in the print field since 2000. Here are three example of how I used my UX intuition and empathy durring those years:

smartdept. inc. (Smith Design) | November 2014
  • A higher education client needed a comprehensive proposal designed and laid out that focused on its history, community involvement, and future investments in order to persuade a foreign government to allow them to build a satellite campus
  • Persona Development- zeroed in on the reader being a knowledgeable businessman looking for detailed and in-depth information stated in a formal business tone with precision and clarity
  • Information Architecture- created the structure and hierarchy of information including positioning for section headers, footers, and tables
  • Visual Design- chose font hierarchy, color scheme, and created tables
smartdept. inc. (Herff Jones | Nystrome) | April 2013- July 2014

The marketing department requested an advertisement designed for the printed program of an educational conference that would drive traffic to their booth.

  • User Reasearch- interviewed a marketing staff member who has manned the company’s conference booths, compile a list of five common goals of attendees
  • Copy Writing- Devised questions from the five goals established from marketing staff interview to use as advertisement copy and created a call to action encouraging attendees to acquire the answers at the company’s booth
  • Visual Design- laid out a full page ad using corporate guidelines
Office Party, The Board Game | August-November 2011

Two women who had conceived a board game needed a prototype developed for play testing, display, and demonstration at the Chicago Toy and Game Fair.

  • Prototyping- Using Adobe Illustrator, designed layout for board, spinner, cards, and pieces; sourced player pawns and hands for spinner; printed and assembled all parts
  • Visual Design- Chose color scheme for visual cues and illustrated pieces
  • User Testing- play tested game with friends age 28 to 35; interviewed each person, asking questions regarding clarity of written rules, game mechanics, level of complexity, and over all enjoyment
  • Results- Client received an invaluable amount of feed back from game experts and was offered a further interview from an investor
